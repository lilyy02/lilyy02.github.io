INTERACTIVE RESEARCH PROJECT

This is a review of the interactive web project "The Man Whose Work We Don't Notice" - available at https://brooomstick.com/

Upon visiting the site, my attention was immediately caught by an animated loading screen. Bold green capital lettering in a sans serif font was centred in my view, with elipses gradually appearing which functioned as a progress bar. Images of different figures also moved across the screen, maintaining my attention as the webpage loaded.

I spent the next two minutes scrolling through the website, being intrigued by all the different animations and transitions between the sections. After I had reached the bottom of the page I returned back to top of the webpage where earlier I had noticed some buttons/links in a horizontal menu. These jumped towards their respective headings on the site which were shown in order as you scrolled. While it was nice to quickly jump to each section, I couldnt find an easy way to quickly jump back to the top of the page where the menu was located.

After doing this I spent some time viewing the page in different size windows, observing that the page adjusted to fit the elements on my screen, however they were not always the same size relative to each other, such as the speech bubbles scaling to be larger relative the rest of the page compared to when I had the window set to fullscreen, 1920x1080. As well as this, I also noticed that the menu dissapeared when the window size was reduced, along with the rubbish that the janitor was seen sweeping up as I scrolled through with a larger browser window.

When interacting with this experience I spent the most time engaging with the single-scroll design of the site, intrigued by how each section was presented in a visually distinct manner despite while still being on the same page. They visual design and animated transtions between each topic covered definitely made the experience more interesting. I found that my most common action in the two minutes I spent interacting with the web experience was scrolling back and forth to view these animations.

Based on the information present, I gathered that the primary goal of this interactive experience was to show apprecitation for janitorial work as well as aim to alter public perception toward the profession to be more positive. It communicates this primary goal through brief facts and images on the history of the janitor profession. As well as this, the webpage raises rhetorical questions to highlight the importance of the role, such as in the heading "What would happen to the planet without the wiper"

I dont believe that this experience should be interacted with on a frequent basis, serving more as a sort of infographic to raise awareness on the importance of janitorial work. I beleive that the web experience is designed to be interacted with only a few times, enough to absorb the information. Rather, I think that it is meant to be shared to others so the message reaches more people. Since the information seems quick and easy to digest for the average person, without going too far into depth, it communicates that it is more of a one-time experience that someone might spend a bit reading about and consider the viewpoints proposed within. It seems intended to prompt more thought about the value of all jobs towards maintaining a functioning society.

The experience references traditional print forms of media such as newspapers, photographs and paintings. The site is styled similar in a way to a scrapbook, with images resembling clippings from old halftone-printed sources with many also being in black and white. The vintage look to these images plays into the idea that in the past janitors were respected for their work. I believe that the references to more traditional forms of media make the experience (and in turn the arguments given by the webpage) feel more grounded in reality, and referencing these as a stylistic choice can make it seem more notable and play on ideas and concepts already familiar to a target audience. These will be ideas I may implement in my own design work in the future. These references also communicate to me a sense of nostalgia for an idealised past, which is somewhat presented by in the sections titled "but that's changed now" and "but that wasn't always the case".

The most frustrating part of the experience was probably the lack of having an easy way to navigate to the different sections after you had already scrolled too far, given that the menu was only at the top of the page and not constantly present. As well as this it seems in smalled window sizes this menu isn't present at all. I also found that some of the points given, such as the example of famous people being janitors and them having been the subject of painting are weak arguments for what I believed this interactive experience's purpose was in inproving common perception of janitors. On the other hand, I found the site to be very well designed in terms of visuals, with the stylistic choices resembling a scrapbook and print media appealing. The animated transitions between different sections were very engaging, and the single-scroll design gave it a nice modern feel contrary to the vintage style images and made reading it feel smooth and uninterrupted.
